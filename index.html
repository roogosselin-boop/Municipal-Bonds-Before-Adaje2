01The Big Picture

Debt has a lifecycle, and the software follows it

A borrower — a university, city, county, or school district — sells bonds to fund a project or to refinance debt it already has. Everything in Adaje maps onto a single, repeating story:

Borrower
Owns the credit & obligation
Project

Purpose
Why money is raised or used
Issue
Bond series inside the project
Debt Service
Principal + interest over time
Manage / Refund
Refinance when it pays

Read these as containers and roles, not just steps: the project holds the work, each purpose says why money is raised or used, and each issue is the actual bond series sold.

Running example · Northgate State University

Northgate built a new science building, funded it with a 2015 bond issue, and has paid debt service ever since. Rates have fallen — so now it's eyeing a refunding. That single arc is the whole module.

Speaker notes

Don't get lost in jargon yet. The one idea to land: every screen in Adaje is a stage in this lifecycle. If someone can place where they are on this line — issuing new debt, or refinancing old debt — the rest of the tool stops feeling abstract.

02Who's Who, What's What

Borrower, issuer, project, and issue

These words get used loosely in conversation. In Adaje they're separate things on purpose — each holds different data, and keeping them apart is what lets you model many scenarios against one credit.

Borrower

The entity responsible for repaying the debt — the obligor. Its credit usually drives the rating. Northgate State University is the borrower here.

Issuer

The legal entity that issues the bonds. Sometimes that's the borrower itself; sometimes a finance authority acts as a conduit issuer on the borrower's behalf.

Project

The broader financing container in Adaje. A single project can hold multiple issues and multiple purposes — not just one deal.

Issue

One financing or bond series inside a project — Series 2015, Series 2026. A project can have several issues; each carries its own maturities, coupons, and pricing.

Conduit issuer — why borrower vs. issuer matters

A finance authority can act as a conduit issuer: it provides the legal mechanism to issue the bonds, but the actual borrower/obligor is usually responsible for repayment. The conduit issuer doesn't necessarily guarantee repayment or lend its own credit support — so the rating typically follows the borrower's credit unless the issuer or another party is actually providing support. That's exactly why Adaje keeps borrower and issuer separate.

One more distinction · issue vs. issuance vs. purpose

An issue is the bond series; issuance is the process of those bonds going to market — don't conflate them. And a purpose (new money or refunding) is not a project. A project can contain multiple issues and purposes when they're part of the same financing scenario, connected through assignments.

Modeling a refunding of live debt

In Adaje, a refunding of existing live debt is generally modeled as its own project. Only one project/instance can be set to Live, and the original debt needs to stay live so its remaining maturities still appear in the borrower-level debt summary. So the original debt and a later refunding usually sit in separate projects.

Speaker notes

The trap is collapsing everything into "the bond." A project is a container for one financing scenario; it can hold several issues and purposes joined by assignments. But a refunding of live debt is its own project — only one instance can be Live, and the original debt must stay live so its remaining maturities still roll up to the borrower-level summary.

03Purposes, Not Projects

New money vs. refunding

Every issue is tied to a purpose — why the bonds are being issued. The two you'll meet most are new money and refunding. These are purposes, not projects. Several purposes and issues can sit in one project when they're part of the same financing scenario — but a refunding of existing live debt is generally its own project.

New money

A purpose where proceeds fund a new project — they buy something that doesn't exist yet (a building, a road, equipment).

Refunding

A purpose where new bonds pay off existing bonds — usually to capture lower rates. Proceeds retire old debt rather than build something new.

Partial refunding

Refunding only part of an old issue — for example, a specific set of maturities — while leaving the rest outstanding.

Northgate, 2015 → 2026

Northgate's Series 2015 bonds were the original debt (a new-money purpose) that built the science center. In 2026, with rates lower, a Series 2026 refunding of the 2030–2035 maturities would be modeled as its own refunding project in Adaje. The original Series 2015 project stays live for the maturities that remain outstanding, while the refunding project shows the new refunding bonds and the refunded maturities.

Speaker notes

Two corrections to make out loud: (1) new money and refunding are purposes, not projects; (2) a refunding of live debt is modeled as a separate project, because the original project must stay live so its remaining maturities keep showing up at the borrower level. New money points proceeds at a project fund; refunding points them at retiring old bonds.

04The Balance That Must Hold

Sources & Uses — the two sides must tie

Sources are the funds available to pay for the Uses. The two totals must match. Below, the columns start out of balance — click Solve and Adaje adds a small rounding amount so they tie, the way the software handles rounding when it sizes a deal.

Sources

Where the money comes from in the financing — such as par amount, premium, funds on hand, or other available funds.

Uses

Where the money goes — such as project fund deposits, refunded bonds, costs, capitalized interest, or reserves.

05Bond Structure

Serial bonds vs. term bonds

An issue is built from maturities. The difference between serial and term is really about payment dates and pricing — not about whether the bonds can be refunded.

Serial bonds

A serial bond has a single payment date on maturity and its own individual pricing — it doesn't share pricing with other payments in the issuance. Serial bonds can mature in any frequency.

MSRB-style: bonds of an issue that mature in consecutive years or other intervals and are not subject to mandatory sinking-fund provisions.

Term bonds

A term bond has at least two payments — sinking-fund payments — that share a single price. Its maturity date falls on the final sinking-fund payment date.

MSRB-style: bonds that come due in a single maturity, with the issuer making periodic sinking-fund payments for mandatory redemption before, or payment at, maturity.

The teaching point

Serial bonds have individual pricing and one payment date; term bonds have multiple sinking-fund payments that share one price. Whether bonds are serial or term doesn't by itself determine whether they can be refunded — it affects how payments and pricing are structured.

Speaker notes

The cleanest framing: serial = one payment date, its own price. Term = several sinking-fund payments under one price, maturing on the last one. Avoid implying that being a term bond makes something refundable — refundability depends on economics, call provisions, and the analysis, not the serial/term label.

06Paying It Back

Amortization and debt service

Amortization is the principal repayment structure of an issue. Debt service is principal plus interest — the actual cash the borrower pays. A common amortization goal is level debt service: principal often increases over time as interest decreases, so total debt service stays relatively level.

Why Adaje users care

For new money, you shape amortization to hit a target debt-service profile (often level). For a refunding, you compare the old debt-service schedule against the new one — the gap between them is the savings. The amortization schedule is the spine both calculations hang on.

Speaker notes

Keep the distinction crisp: amortization is principal only; debt service is principal + interest. Refunding savings are literally old debt service minus new debt service, year by year. That's why the schedule is the first thing analysts look at.

07Pricing Mechanics

Coupon, yield, price, and premium / discount

Four words that trip people up — and one pair, premium / discount, that follows from the others.

Coupon

The stated interest rate on the bond. It sets the interest paid, based on par. In conduit deals, the borrower/obligor is usually the party responsible for repayment.

Yield

The investor's return given what they paid — essentially the market rate.

Price

What investors actually pay, quoted vs. par. 100 = par; 110 = 110% of face.

Premium / Discount

Any amount paid above par is premium; any amount below par is discount.

What drives it

Premium and discount are results of the relationship between coupon, yield, price, delivery date, and call structure — not inputs you set directly. As a rule of thumb, when the coupon is higher than the yield, the price is above 100 (a premium). Example: a Northgate maturity with a 5.00% coupon priced to yield 3.50% might price near 110, and that premium shows up as proceeds in Sources & Uses.

Speaker notes

Coupon is what the issuer pays; yield is what the investor earns. Above par = premium, below par = discount. The important nuance: premium/discount are outputs of coupon, yield, price, delivery date, and call structure — so they move when any of those move.

08When Bonds Can Be Redeemed

Call dates, redemption, and defeasance

A call date affects when existing bonds can be redeemed at the stated call price — it doesn't, by itself, decide whether a bond gets refunded. Optional redemption is that right in action.

Call date & price

On or after the call date, bonds can be redeemed at the call price — often 100% of par at first call.

Defeasance

Bonds may be defeased before the call date by setting aside funds in an escrow — though they usually can't be redeemed at the normal call price until that date.

Make-whole

Before the call date, some bonds carry a make-whole provision — they can be redeemed earlier, but at a formula-based make-whole price rather than the normal call price.

Simple example

Old bonds are callable on 11/1/2034 at 100%. That date sets when they can be redeemed at the call price. Not all callable bonds are refunded, though — whether one is refunded depends on economics, structure, call provisions, and the purpose of the analysis.

Speaker notes

Correct the common misconception: the call date governs when redemption at the call price is allowed — not whether a bond can be refunded. Defeasance (escrow) and make-whole provisions are the two ways activity can happen before the call date. Whether a refunding actually happens is an economic and structural question.

09Putting It Together

Refunding analysis

This is where the earlier ideas combine. A refunding asks one question: does issuing new bonds to retire old ones save enough, today, to be worth doing?

Old vs. new debt service

The core comparison — line up the existing debt service against the proposed new debt service.

Refunded par

The face amount of old bonds being retired (Northgate's 2030–2035 maturities: ~$12M).

PV savings

Present value of the debt-service reduction — the headline benefit (illustratively ~$640K).

Call date / redemption timing

When the old bonds can be redeemed at the call price — the timing the analysis works around.

Match maturity

Structuring the new bonds to correspond to the maturities being refunded — useful for comparing savings.

"Match maturity," explained

A match-maturity refunding structures the new bonds to correspond to the maturities being refunded, with the final maturity of the refunding bonds lined up to the final maturity being refunded. That lets you lay the old debt service next to the new debt service for a cleaner apples-to-apples read on savings. It's a useful structure for comparison — not something that's always required.

Speaker notes

Keep it practical: compare old debt service to new debt service, look at PV savings on the refunded par, and respect the call date / redemption timing. Match maturity makes the comparison cleaner. Don't dwell on costs of issuance or negative arbitrage here.
